YouTube vs The Media: It’s Time Throw In The Towel

I messed up. It’s not a phrase you hear enormously often from members of the mainstream media. The tabloids in particular are fond of splashing an inflammatory headline on the front-page, then issuing a half-hearted apology on page 10 a week later when the possibility of a lawsuit arises. I’ve not written anything that’s likely to land me in court — and I’ve certainly no intention of issuing a forced, perfunctory apology — but nonetheless, I feel compelled to say it: I messed up.

Earlier this year, I began to become interesting in the YouTube community, and in particular its spectrum of exciting, young, and somewhat undiscovered (at least by the wider public) musicians. I got the fizzing sense of bubbling excitement, the kind of enthusiasm that normally inspires my best work, to the extent that I began pestering the editors of music websites to let me write about them, and eventually one prominent example said yes, despite my lack of experience in the genre. It didn’t take long for me to then line up an interview with Dodie Clark, in which I nervously and excitedly stumbled through a bunch of questions I’d prepared weeks in advance. In return, she answered openly, honestly, and intelligently, leaving me so enthused to coalesce my many scrambled thoughts into a insightful and thoughtful look into what makes a YouTube creator tick.

That was the plan, anyway. After successive edits of my giddy draft, some of which I was involved with, others that I wasn’t, everything fell apart. A paragraph exploring the benefits of a creative collective intertwining their respective arts online hit the cutting room floor, replaced with a series of sneering comments about monetising a tween audience. Slowly but surely, my positive original version morphed into something far less upbeat. When the piece went live, I was pretty gutted, and initially quite angry with the editorial team who’d slapped an incendiary title on a snark-ified version of my words. But in truth, this was my failure. I’d seen the direction it was headed, and though I pushed back where I felt able, my eagerness and excitement to share this community with a wider audience meant that I didn’t do nearly enough. In some misplaced sense of journalistic integrity, I worried I was too invested in the featured artists, too keen to showcase them, and somehow felt that I’d be doing my readership a disservice by not letting the editors infuse their, albeit negative, views too.

The end result was about one and a half thousand words that make me quite sad. I’ve never had the sensation of seeing my name attached to a piece of work that I profoundly disagree with, and I’m not keen to repeat it. I tried, unsuccessfully, to have the piece pulled, I didn’t take any payment for it, and its existence has played on my mind almost every day since. I let down a remarkable yet oft-dismissed community whilst trying to shine the spotlight on them. I chatted frankly and candidly with a passionate and talented creator whose heart-wrenching songs and intimate vlogs had genuinely touched me, and allowed her to be thrown under the bus. And I enabled tedious, untrue stereotypes to be perpetuated under my name. I messed up.

But I think there’s more here than just a lesson for me in how to choose publications and manage editors. In the wake of similar negative stories, the YouTube community has often lashed back — just recently, an Guardian piece blaming Zoella for illiteracy sparked outrage and response videos in equal measures. It all plays into this bizarre war between YouTube and “the media” (which, by the way is a baffling term; bundling Knitting Weekly’s editor in with Piers Morgan is as daft as conflating Sam Pepper and Zoella). There’s a tendency on both sides to jump to extremes: YouTubers are either a brain-rotting plague on our youth, or bastions of new media here to save the world an out-of-touch older generation. Similarly, the mainstream media is either a redundant, ignorant, dying art, or the last line of defense against vapid, clickbait-y nonsense. These easy, over-emotive caricatures do nobody any favours, and with so much in the world worth getting angry about, having traditional media and YouTubers butting heads is a pointless, fruitless exercise in stubbornness.

That I tried to write an uplifting piece about YouTube culture and ended up peddling anti-Youtube rhetoric, says a lot about the de-facto antagonistic standard. That’s not to say that media outlets and YouTubers must always sing each other’s praises, just that the automatic hostility leads to reductive, repetitive arguments. There is, of course, place for the media and YouTubers to cross-critique: critics form an integral part of the film, television, and creative arts communities, and if YouTube wishes to stand alongside them, then it must be prepared to face honest appraisals. Conversely, if the mainstream media ever wants to get more from the Youtube community than a pantomime villain to boo and hiss at, it needs to engage knowledgeably. It can be critical, scathing even, but it must trade in fact, rather than stereotype.

It’s on us as writers to delve beyond the candy videos and pranks to connect meaningfully with YouTube’s range of diverse, intimate and unique content and creators. To do careful research and avoid slipping into tired cliches that don’t represent the whole. And even, to critique YouTubers as we would film, TV, or song — but this must come from a place of knowledge and understanding, rather than one of ignorance.

It’s on YouTubers to help a sceptical press to see past the cliches to the amazing, interdisciplinary art produced on their home platform. To accept that criticism will come (some fair and valid, some not) and to not stoke the anti-media flames when it does. And to play their part in starving ill-informed or spiteful writing of an audience by not sharing or engaging with it. Doing so is playing into the hands of that publication: there’s no distinction between clicks of agreement or clicks of morbid curiosity, when it comes to ad revenue.

Finally, it’s on all of us to do better than slip into the sweeping generalisations that have largely defined the press-YouTuber relationship thus far. To recognise that YouTube creators and journalists alike are not warring tribes, but individuals, who may not wish to be lumped wholesale with every part of their broader community. And to build a healthy and symbiotic relationship, because our mediums are at their best when we’re learning from — rather than needlessly attacking — each other.
